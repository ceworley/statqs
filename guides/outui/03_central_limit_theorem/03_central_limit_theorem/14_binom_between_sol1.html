We determine the mean and standard deviation of the binomial distribution. \[\mu ~=~ np ~=~ (64)(0.4) ~=~ 25.6\] \[\sigma ~=~ \sqrt{np(1-p)} ~=~ \sqrt{(64)(0.4)(0.6)} ~=~ 3.9191836 \]

We determine both \(z\)-scores. (In the de Moivre-Laplace notes, I used \(\sum x\) to emphasize that a binomial variable is a sum of Bernoulli trials. Here, I will just use \(x\) as the boundary for number of successes, because this is the more common notation.) We get the first \(z\)-score.

\[\begin{aligned} z_1 &= \frac{x_1 - \mu}{\sigma} \\\\ &= \frac{28.5-25.6}{3.9191836} \\\\ &= 0.73995 \end{aligned}\]

We get the second \(z\)-score.

\[\begin{aligned} z_2 &= \frac{x_2 - \mu}{\sigma} \\\\ &= \frac{29.5-25.6}{3.9191836} \\\\ &= 0.9951052 \end{aligned}\]

Then, find the standard normal probability.

\[P(0.73995 < Z < 0.9951052) ~=~ 0.0698226 \]

Of course, you could have rounded the \(z\)-scores, so any of the following will also get credit. \[P(0.73 < Z < 0.99) ~=~ 0.0716 \] \[P(0.74 < Z < 1) ~=~ 0.0709 \] \[P(0.73 < Z < 1) ~=~ 0.0685 \] \[P(0.74 < Z < 0.99) ~=~ 0.074 \]
