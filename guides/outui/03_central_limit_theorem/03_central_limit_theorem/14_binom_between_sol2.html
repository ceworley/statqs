We determine the mean and standard deviation of the binomial distribution. \[\mu ~=~ np ~=~ (183)(0.77) ~=~ 140.91\] \[\sigma ~=~ \sqrt{np(1-p)} ~=~ \sqrt{(183)(0.77)(0.23)} ~=~ 5.6929167 \]

We determine both \(z\)-scores. (In the de Moivre-Laplace notes, I used \(\sum x\) to emphasize that a binomial variable is a sum of Bernoulli trials. Here, I will just use \(x\) as the boundary for number of successes, because this is the more common notation.) We get the first \(z\)-score.

\[\begin{aligned} z_1 &= \frac{x_1 - \mu}{\sigma} \\\\ &= \frac{133.5-140.91}{5.6929167} \\\\ &= -1.3016175 \end{aligned}\]

We get the second \(z\)-score.

\[\begin{aligned} z_2 &= \frac{x_2 - \mu}{\sigma} \\\\ &= \frac{148.5-140.91}{5.6929167} \\\\ &= 1.3332357 \end{aligned}\]

Then, find the standard normal probability.

\[P(-1.3016175 < Z < 1.3332357) ~=~ 0.8122492 \]

Of course, you could have rounded the \(z\)-scores, so any of the following will also get credit. \[P(-1.31 < Z < 1.33) ~=~ 0.8131 \] \[P(-1.3 < Z < 1.34) ~=~ 0.8131 \] \[P(-1.31 < Z < 1.34) ~=~ 0.8114 \] \[P(-1.3 < Z < 1.33) ~=~ 0.8148 \]
