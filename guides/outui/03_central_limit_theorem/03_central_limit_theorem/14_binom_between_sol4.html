We determine the mean and standard deviation of the binomial distribution. \[\mu ~=~ np ~=~ (165)(0.63) ~=~ 103.95\] \[\sigma ~=~ \sqrt{np(1-p)} ~=~ \sqrt{(165)(0.63)(0.37)} ~=~ 6.2017336 \]

We determine both \(z\)-scores. (In the de Moivre-Laplace notes, I used \(\sum x\) to emphasize that a binomial variable is a sum of Bernoulli trials. Here, I will just use \(x\) as the boundary for number of successes, because this is the more common notation.) We get the first \(z\)-score.

\[\begin{aligned} z_1 &= \frac{x_1 - \mu}{\sigma} \\\\ &= \frac{103.5-103.95}{6.2017336} \\\\ &= -0.0725604 \end{aligned}\]

We get the second \(z\)-score.

\[\begin{aligned} z_2 &= \frac{x_2 - \mu}{\sigma} \\\\ &= \frac{107.5-103.95}{6.2017336} \\\\ &= 0.5724206 \end{aligned}\]

Then, find the standard normal probability.

\[P(-0.0725604 < Z < 0.5724206) ~=~ 0.2454035 \]

Of course, you could have rounded the \(z\)-scores, so any of the following will also get credit. \[P(-0.08 < Z < 0.57) ~=~ 0.2476 \] \[P(-0.07 < Z < 0.58) ~=~ 0.2469 \] \[P(-0.08 < Z < 0.58) ~=~ 0.2436 \] \[P(-0.07 < Z < 0.57) ~=~ 0.2509 \]
