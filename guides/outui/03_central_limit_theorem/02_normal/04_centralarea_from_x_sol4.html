First, draw a sketch. We can label the \(M\) axis by adding integer multiples of 1 to 11. We know to shade the center because values near 11 satisfy the condition \(\big|M-11\big| < 2\). We draw the boundaries at \(11-2=9\) and \(11+2=13\) because those are the solutions to \(\big|M-11\big| = 2\). We can also rephrase the probability.

\[P\left(\big|M-11\big| < 2\right) ~=~ P(9<M<13)\]


We calculate the \(z\) values of the boundaries. Left boundary: \[\begin{aligned} z_1 &= \frac{9-11}{1} \\\\ &= \frac{-2}{1} \\\\ &= -2 \end{aligned}\]

Right boundary:

\[\begin{aligned} z_2 &= \frac{13-11}{1} \\\\ &= \frac{2}{1} \\\\ &= 2 \end{aligned}\]

We have rephrased our problem into a standard normal probability problem, because

\[P\left(\big|M-11\big| < 2\right) ~=~ P(9<M<13) ~=~ P(-2<Z<2) ~=~ P\left(\big|Z\big|<2\right)\]

So, we just need to evaluate \(P(|Z|<2)\). I will also point out that \(\frac{2}{1} = 2\). In general, if \(X\) is normally distributed, then: \[P\left(\big|X-\mu\big|<d\right) ~=~ P\left(\big|Z\big| < \frac{d}{\sigma}\right)\]

From here we have a formula that lets us use the \(z\) table. (We practiced this part before.) \[\begin{aligned} P(|Z|<2) &= 2 \times P(Z<2)-1 \\ &= 2 \times 0.9772-1 \\ &= 0.9544 \end{aligned}\]
