The formula to determine the \(z\)-score of a measurement is the ratio with numerator the difference between measurement and population mean and denominator the population standard deviation.

\[z = \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \]

The highest \(z\)-score (furthest right on number line) corresponds to the most unusually large measurement.

The smallest \(z\)-score (furthest left on number line) corresponds to the most unusually small measurement.

The smallest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most usually sized measurement.

The largest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most unusually sized measurement.


  1. We use the formula for calculating the kiwi specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{106.8-101}{7} = 0.83 \]
  2. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<0.83) = 0.7967 \]
  3. We use the formula for calculating the pineapple specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{1992-1985}{120} = 0.06 \]
  4. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<0.06) = 0.5239 \]
  5. We use the formula for calculating the apricot specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{57.32-63}{4} = -1.42 \]
  6. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<-1.42) = 0.0778 \]
  7. We use the formula for calculating the grapefruit specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{195.7-223}{21} = -1.3 \]
  8. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<-1.3) = 0.0968 \]
  9. We determine the maximum \(z\) score is \(0.83\), which belongs to the kiwi. / . / . / .
  10. We determine the minimum \(z\) score is \(-1.42\), which belongs to the apricot. / . / . / .
  11. We determine the minimum absolute \(z\) score is \(0.06\), which belongs to the pineapple. / . / . / .
  12. We determine the maximum absolute \(z\) score is \(1.42\), which belongs to the apricot. / . / . / .
