First, draw a sketch. We can label the \(J\) axis by adding integer multiples of 20 to 110. We know to shade toward the right because large values of \(J\) satisfy the condition \(J>120\).


We are given a specific \(j\) value as a boundary. (Remember, for random variables we use uppercase letters, but for specific values we use lowercase.) \[j = 120 \] We calculate the \(z\) value of the boundary. \[\begin{aligned} z &= \frac{j-\mu}{\sigma} \\\\ &= \frac{120-110}{20} \\\\ &= 0.5 \end{aligned}\]

We have rephrased our problem into a standard normal probability problem, because \[P(J>120) ~=~ P(Z>0.5) \]

So, we just need to evaluate \(P(Z>0.5)\).

To do this, you need to remember that right-area events are complementary to left-area events. \[P(Z>0.5) ~=~ 1-P(Z<0.5) \]

You can use a \(z\)-table.

\(z\) \(P(Z<z)\)
0.48 0.6844
0.49 0.6879
0.5 0.6915
0.51 0.695
0.52 0.6985

Thus, we find our answer. \[\begin{aligned} P(J>120) &= 1-0.6915 \\ &= 0.3085 \end{aligned}\]
