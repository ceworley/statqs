You may find it helpful to convert the densities to frequencies by multiplying each density by both the total sample size (\(n=500\)) and the width of the bar (5).


The first 4 questions involve adding up frequencies of the indicated intervals. The last 4 questions can be done by guessing and checking until something works.


  1. (ANS=0.58) There are 290 measurements less than 40. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<40]=0.58\).
  2. (ANS=0.47) There are 235 measurements more than 35. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>35]=0.47\).
  3. (ANS=0.07) There are 35 measurements closer than 2.5 units from 42.5. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-42.5|<2.5]=0.07\).
  4. (ANS=0.904) There are 452 measurements further than 5 units from 35. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-35|>5]=0.904\).
  5. (ANS=45) A boundary at \(b=45\) has 325 measurements less than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<45]=0.65\).
  6. (ANS=25) A boundary at \(b=25\) has 296 measurements more than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>25]=0.592\).
  7. (ANS=2.5) An interval with radius \(r=2.5\) around center 32.5 contains 23 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-32.5|<2.5]=0.046\).
  8. (ANS=7.5) An interval with radius \(r=7.5\) around center 32.5 excludes 414 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-32.5|>7.5]=0.828\).
