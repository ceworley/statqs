You may find it helpful to convert the densities to frequencies by multiplying each density by both the total sample size (\(n=200\)) and the width of the bar (5).


The first 4 questions involve adding up frequencies of the indicated intervals. The last 4 questions can be done by guessing and checking until something works.


  1. (ANS=0.61) There are 122 measurements less than 40. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<40]=0.61\).
  2. (ANS=0.595) There are 119 measurements more than 35. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>35]=0.595\).
  3. (ANS=0.405) There are 81 measurements closer than 5 units from 40. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-40|<5]=0.405\).
  4. (ANS=0.795) There are 159 measurements further than 2.5 units from 37.5. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-37.5|>2.5]=0.795\).
  5. (ANS=40) A boundary at \(b=40\) has 122 measurements less than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<40]=0.61\).
  6. (ANS=40) A boundary at \(b=40\) has 78 measurements more than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>40]=0.39\).
  7. (ANS=2.5) An interval with radius \(r=2.5\) around center 42.5 contains 40 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-42.5|<2.5]=0.2\).
  8. (ANS=2.5) An interval with radius \(r=2.5\) around center 42.5 excludes 160 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-42.5|>2.5]=0.8\).
