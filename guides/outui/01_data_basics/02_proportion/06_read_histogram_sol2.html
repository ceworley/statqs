You may find it helpful to convert the densities to frequencies by multiplying each density by both the total sample size (\(n=200\)) and the width of the bar (1).


The first 4 questions involve adding up frequencies of the indicated intervals. The last 4 questions can be done by guessing and checking until something works.


  1. (ANS=0.135) There are 27 measurements less than 40. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<40]=0.135\).
  2. (ANS=0.985) There are 197 measurements more than 37. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>37]=0.985\).
  3. (ANS=0.37) There are 74 measurements closer than 1.5 units from 42.5. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-42.5|<1.5]=0.37\).
  4. (ANS=0.935) There are 187 measurements further than 0.5 units from 40.5. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-40.5|>0.5]=0.935\).
  5. (ANS=43) A boundary at \(b=43\) has 83 measurements less than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<43]=0.415\).
  6. (ANS=40) A boundary at \(b=40\) has 173 measurements more than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>40]=0.865\).
  7. (ANS=3) An interval with radius \(r=3\) around center 41 contains 107 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-41|<3]=0.535\).
  8. (ANS=3.5) An interval with radius \(r=3.5\) around center 40.5 excludes 89 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-40.5|>3.5]=0.445\).
