You may find it helpful to convert the densities to frequencies by multiplying each density by both the total sample size (\(n=200\)) and the width of the bar (2).


The first 4 questions involve adding up frequencies of the indicated intervals. The last 4 questions can be done by guessing and checking until something works.


  1. (ANS=0.66) There are 132 measurements less than 64. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<64]=0.66\).
  2. (ANS=0.425) There are 85 measurements more than 62. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>62]=0.425\).
  3. (ANS=0.195) There are 39 measurements closer than 2 units from 58. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-58|<2]=0.195\).
  4. (ANS=0.86) There are 172 measurements further than 2 units from 62. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-62|>2]=0.86\).
  5. (ANS=64) A boundary at \(b=64\) has 132 measurements less than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<64]=0.66\).
  6. (ANS=60) A boundary at \(b=60\) has 96 measurements more than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>60]=0.48\).
  7. (ANS=1) An interval with radius \(r=1\) around center 59 contains 16 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-59|<1]=0.08\).
  8. (ANS=3) An interval with radius \(r=3\) around center 59 excludes 150 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-59|>3]=0.75\).

ANSWERS: 0.66, 0.425, 0.195, 0.86, 64, 60, 1, 3
