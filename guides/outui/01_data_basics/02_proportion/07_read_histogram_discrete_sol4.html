The first 4 questions involve adding up relative frequencies of the indicated intervals. The last 4 questions can be done by guessing and checking until something works.

You may also find it is helpful to determine frequencies (counts). You do this by multiplying each relative frequency by the total number of measurements.



  1. (ANS=0.484) There are 242 measurements less than 3.5. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<3.5]=\frac{242}{500}=0.484\).
  2. (ANS=0.516) There are 258 measurements more than 3.5. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>3.5]=\frac{258}{500}=0.516\).
  3. (ANS=0.142) There are 71 measurements closer than 0.5 units from 5. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-5|<0.5]=\frac{71}{500}=0.142\).
  4. (ANS=0.686) There are 343 measurements further than 1 units from 2.5. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-2.5|>1]=\frac{343}{500}=0.686\).
  5. (ANS=4.5) First, convert the proportion into a count. \(0.684\cdot500=342\). A boundary at \(b=4.5\) has 342 measurements less than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<4.5]=0.684\).
  6. (ANS=4.5) First, convert the proportion into a count. \(0.316\cdot500=158\). A boundary at \(b=4.5\) has 158 measurements more than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>4.5]=0.316\).
  7. (ANS=1) An interval with radius \(r=1\) around center 2.5 contains 157 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-2.5|<1]=0.314\).
  8. (ANS=1) An interval with radius \(r=1\) around center 2.5 excludes 343 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-2.5|>1]=0.686\).
