Background

Sometimes a population is well characterized, meaning we know its mean and standard deviation. We use greek letters \(\mu\) (“mu”) and \(\sigma\) (“sigma”) when describing the population (instead of the \(\bar{x}\) (“xbar”) and \(s\) used for sample mean and sample standard deviation). \[\mu = \text{population mean} \] \[\sigma = \text{population standard deviation} \]

When measuring individuals from a population, we expect most measurements to be within the interval of typical measurements. We will define the interval of typical measurements (using interval notation): \[\text{interval of typical measurements} = (\mu-2\sigma,\,\mu+2\sigma) \] In other words, we expect most measurements to be between two bounds. \[\text{lower bound of interval of typical measurements} = \mu-2\sigma \] \[\text{upper bound of interval of typical measurements} = \mu+2\sigma \]

Actual Question

A population of lizards has a mean length of \(\mu = 24.6\) cm and a standard deviation of \(\sigma=4.1\) cm. Determine the interval of typical measurements.


  1. Determine the lower bound of the interval of typical measurements.
  2. Determine the upper bound of the interval of typical measurements.
