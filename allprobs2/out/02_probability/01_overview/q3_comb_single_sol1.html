This scenario describes a combinations problem (order of selection does not matter). We are considering the subsets of size 5 from a set of size 11.

nCr=n!(n−r)!⋅r!n=11r=511C5=11!(11−5)!⋅5!=11!6!⋅5!=11⋅10⋅9⋅8⋅75⋅4⋅3⋅2⋅1=462\begin{aligned} {_nC_r} &= \frac{n!}{(n-r)! \cdot r!} \\\\ n &= 11 \\\\ r &= 5 \\\\ {_{11}C_{5}} &= \frac{11!}{(11-5)!\cdot 5! } \\\\ &= \frac{11!}{6! \cdot 5!} \\\\ &= \frac{ 11\cdot10\cdot9\cdot8\cdot7}{ 5\cdot4\cdot3\cdot2\cdot1} \\\\ &= 462 \end{aligned}

Remember, we care about combinations because they represent all the ways we can select rr 1s and n−rn-r 0s. So, in this case, 11C5=462{_{11}C_{5}} = 462 tells us there are 462 ways of selecting 6 0s and 5 1s. (Think of the 1s as the toppings that are selected and 0s as the toppings NOT selected.)
