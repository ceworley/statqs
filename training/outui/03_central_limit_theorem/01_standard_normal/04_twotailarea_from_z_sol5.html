First, you need to identify that we are looking for a two-tail area (the sum of the left and right tails). This is because \(z\)-scores far from 0 satisfy \(|Z|>1.34\), while \(z\)-scores near 0 do not satisfy the inequality.

Start with a sketch.


Remember, the entire area is always 1. Most z-tables only provides left areas, so three methods are shown to get the two-tail area from a left-area (cumulative probability) table.

Method 1: First find the area of the left tail. \[P(Z<-1.34) = 0.0901\] Recognize normal distributions are symmetric, so we also know the area of the right tail. \[P(Z>1.34) = 0.0901 \] The two areas add to our desired two-tail area. \[\begin{aligned} P\left(|Z|>1.34\right) &= P(Z<-1.34)+P(Z>1.34) \\ &= 0.0901 + 0.0901 \\ &= 0.1802 \end{aligned}\]

This technique can be summarized with the following formula: \[P\left(|Z|>z\right) = 2\cdot P(Z<-z)\] assuming \(z>0\).

So, in our case (when \(z=1.34\)), \[\begin{aligned} P\left(|Z|<1.34\right) &= 2\cdot P(Z<-1.34) \\ &= 2\cdot0.0901 \\ &= 0.1802 \end{aligned}\]

This method is shown graphically:


Notice we need to add both tails.

Method 2: You can achieve the same result by using the following formula: \[P\left(|Z|>z\right) = 2\cdot\left[1-P(Z<z) \right] \] So, \[\begin{aligned} P\left(|Z|>1.34\right) &= 2\cdot\left[1-P(Z<1.34) \right] \\ &= 2\cdot\left[1-0.9099\right] \\ &= 2\cdot0.0901\\ &= 0.1802 \end{aligned}\]

It might be helpful to visualize with a spinner.


In a spreadsheet, you could use the NORM.DIST() function.

=2*NORM.DIST(-1.34,0,1,TRUE)

In R, you could use the pnorm function.

2*pnorm(-1.34)
