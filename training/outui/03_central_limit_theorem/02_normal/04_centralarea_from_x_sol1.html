First, draw a sketch. We can label the \(R\) axis by adding integer multiples of 8 to 31. We know to shade the center because values near 31 satisfy the condition \(\big|R-31\big| < 12\). We draw the boundaries at \(31-12=19\) and \(31+12=43\) because those are the solutions to \(\big|R-31\big| = 12\). We can also rephrase the probability.

\[P\left(\big|R-31\big| < 12\right) ~=~ P(19<R<43)\]


We calculate the \(z\) values of the boundaries. Left boundary: \[\begin{aligned} z_1 &= \frac{19-31}{8} \\\\ &= \frac{-12}{8} \\\\ &= -1.5 \end{aligned}\]

Right boundary:

\[\begin{aligned} z_2 &= \frac{43-31}{8} \\\\ &= \frac{12}{8} \\\\ &= 1.5 \end{aligned}\]

We have rephrased our problem into a standard normal probability problem, because

\[P\left(\big|R-31\big| < 12\right) ~=~ P(19<R<43) ~=~ P(-1.5<Z<1.5) ~=~ P\left(\big|Z\big|<1.5\right)\]

So, we just need to evaluate \(P(|Z|<1.5)\). I will also point out that \(\frac{12}{8} = 1.5\). In general, if \(X\) is normally distributed, then: \[P\left(\big|X-\mu\big|<d\right) ~=~ P\left(\big|Z\big| < \frac{d}{\sigma}\right)\]

From here we have a formula that lets us use the \(z\) table. (We practiced this part before.) \[\begin{aligned} P(|Z|<1.5) &= 2 \times P(Z<1.5)-1 \\ &= 2 \times 0.9332-1 \\ &= 0.8664 \end{aligned}\]
