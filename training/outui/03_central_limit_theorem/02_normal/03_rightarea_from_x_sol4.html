First, draw a sketch. We can label the \(C\) axis by adding integer multiples of 0.6 to 2.2. We know to shade toward the right because large values of \(C\) satisfy the condition \(C>3.1\).


We are given a specific \(c\) value as a boundary. (Remember, for random variables we use uppercase letters, but for specific values we use lowercase.) \[c = 3.1 \] We calculate the \(z\) value of the boundary. \[\begin{aligned} z &= \frac{c-\mu}{\sigma} \\\\ &= \frac{3.1-2.2}{0.6} \\\\ &= 1.5 \end{aligned}\]

We have rephrased our problem into a standard normal probability problem, because \[P(C>3.1) ~=~ P(Z>1.5) \]

So, we just need to evaluate \(P(Z>1.5)\).

To do this, you need to remember that right-area events are complementary to left-area events. \[P(Z>1.5) ~=~ 1-P(Z<1.5) \]

You can use a \(z\)-table.

\(z\) \(P(Z<z)\)

1.49 0.9319
1.5 0.9332
1.51 0.9345
1.52 0.9357

Thus, we find our answer. \[\begin{aligned} P(C>3.1) &= 1-0.9332 \\ &= 0.0668 \end{aligned}\]
