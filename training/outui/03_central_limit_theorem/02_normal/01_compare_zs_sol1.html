The formula to determine the \(z\)-score of a measurement is the ratio with numerator the difference between measurement and population mean and denominator the population standard deviation.

\[z = \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \]

The highest \(z\)-score (furthest right on number line) corresponds to the most unusually large measurement.

The smallest \(z\)-score (furthest left on number line) corresponds to the most unusually small measurement.

The smallest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most usually sized measurement.

The largest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most unusually sized measurement.


  1. We use the formula for calculating the banana specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{214.5-194}{12} = 1.71 \]
  2. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<1.71) = 0.9564 \]
  3. We use the formula for calculating the pineapple specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{2437-2507}{134} = -0.52 \]
  4. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<-0.52) = 0.3015 \]
  5. We use the formula for calculating the apricot specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{39.36-39}{2} = 0.18 \]
  6. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<0.18) = 0.5714 \]
  7. We use the formula for calculating the coconut specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{628.9-722}{70} = -1.33 \]
  8. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<-1.33) = 0.0918 \]
  9. We determine the maximum \(z\) score is \(1.71\), which belongs to the banana. / . / . / .
  10. We determine the minimum \(z\) score is \(-1.33\), which belongs to the coconut. / . / . / .
  11. We determine the minimum absolute \(z\) score is \(0.18\), which belongs to the apricot. / . / . / .
  12. We determine the maximum absolute \(z\) score is \(1.71\), which belongs to the banana. / . / . / .
