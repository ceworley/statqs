First, draw a sketch. We can label the \(K\) axis by adding integer multiples of 12 to 61. We know to shade the center because values near 61 satisfy the condition \(\big|K-61\big| < 12\). We draw the boundaries at \(61-12=49\) and \(61+12=73\) because those are the solutions to \(\big|K-61\big| = 12\). We can also rephrase the probability.

\[P\left(\big|K-61\big| < 12\right) ~=~ P(49<K<73)\]


We calculate the \(z\) values of the boundaries. Left boundary: \[\begin{aligned} z_1 &= \frac{49-61}{12} \\\\ &= \frac{-12}{12} \\\\ &= -1 \end{aligned}\]

Right boundary:

\[\begin{aligned} z_2 &= \frac{73-61}{12} \\\\ &= \frac{12}{12} \\\\ &= 1 \end{aligned}\]

We have rephrased our problem into a standard normal probability problem, because

\[P\left(\big|K-61\big| < 12\right) ~=~ P(49<K<73) ~=~ P(-1<Z<1) ~=~ P\left(\big|Z\big|<1\right)\]

So, we just need to evaluate \(P(|Z|<1)\). I will also point out that \(\frac{12}{12} = 1\). In general, if \(X\) is normally distributed, then: \[P\left(\big|X-\mu\big|<d\right) ~=~ P\left(\big|Z\big| < \frac{d}{\sigma}\right)\]

From here we have a formula that lets us use the \(z\) table. (We practiced this part before.) \[\begin{aligned} P(|Z|<1) &= 2 \times P(Z<1)-1 \\ &= 2 \times 0.8413-1 \\ &= 0.6826 \end{aligned}\]
