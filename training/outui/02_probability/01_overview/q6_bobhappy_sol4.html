Use the definition of conditional probability. \[P(\text{win given happy}) = \frac{P(\text{win and happy})}{P(\text{happy})}\] We can first determine all the joint probabilities. \[P(A\text{ and }B) = P(A)\cdot P(B\text{ given } A) \] \[\begin{aligned} P(\text{win and happy}) &= 0.69\times0.44 = 0.3036\\ P(\text{win and sad}) &= 0.69\times(1-0.44) = 0.3864\\ P(\text{lose and happy}) &= (1-0.69)\times0.11 = 0.0341\\ P(\text{lose and sad}) &= (1-0.69)\times(1-0.11) = 0.2759\\ \end{aligned}\]

Notice there are two disjoint ways Bob could be happy. \[\begin{aligned} P(\text{happy}) &= P\left(\text{[win and happy] OR [lose and happy]}\right) \\ &= P(\text{win and happy}) + P(\text{lose and happy}) \\ &= 0.3036+0.0341 \\ &= 0.3377 \end{aligned}\] So, back to the conditional probability. \[P(\text{win given happy}) = \frac{0.3036}{0.3377} = 0.899\]
