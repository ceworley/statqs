Use the definition of conditional probability. \[P(\text{win given happy}) = \frac{P(\text{win and happy})}{P(\text{happy})}\] We can first determine all the joint probabilities. \[P(A\text{ and }B) = P(A)\cdot P(B\text{ given } A) \] \[\begin{aligned} P(\text{win and happy}) &= 0.22\times0.79 = 0.1738\\ P(\text{win and sad}) &= 0.22\times(1-0.79) = 0.0462\\ P(\text{lose and happy}) &= (1-0.22)\times0.18 = 0.1404\\ P(\text{lose and sad}) &= (1-0.22)\times(1-0.18) = 0.6396\\ \end{aligned}\]

Notice there are two disjoint ways Bob could be happy. \[\begin{aligned} P(\text{happy}) &= P\left(\text{[win and happy] OR [lose and happy]}\right) \\ &= P(\text{win and happy}) + P(\text{lose and happy}) \\ &= 0.1738+0.1404 \\ &= 0.3142 \end{aligned}\] So, back to the conditional probability. \[P(\text{win given happy}) = \frac{0.1738}{0.3142} = 0.553\]
