Use the definition of conditional probability. \[P(\text{win given happy}) = \frac{P(\text{win and happy})}{P(\text{happy})}\] We can first determine all the joint probabilities. \[P(A\text{ and }B) = P(A)\cdot P(B\text{ given } A) \] \[\begin{aligned} P(\text{win and happy}) &= 0.34\times0.59 = 0.2006\\ P(\text{win and sad}) &= 0.34\times(1-0.59) = 0.1394\\ P(\text{lose and happy}) &= (1-0.34)\times0.35 = 0.231\\ P(\text{lose and sad}) &= (1-0.34)\times(1-0.35) = 0.429\\ \end{aligned}\]

Notice there are two disjoint ways Bob could be happy. \[\begin{aligned} P(\text{happy}) &= P\left(\text{[win and happy] OR [lose and happy]}\right) \\ &= P(\text{win and happy}) + P(\text{lose and happy}) \\ &= 0.2006+0.231 \\ &= 0.4316 \end{aligned}\] So, back to the conditional probability. \[P(\text{win given happy}) = \frac{0.2006}{0.4316} = 0.465\]
