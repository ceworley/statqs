Intervals (review)

  • A half-bounded interval has 1 boundary.
    • \(x<10\)

    • \(x\ge20\)

  • A bounded interval has 2 boundaries.
    • \(-25\le x ~~~\mathtt{AND}~~~ x<40\)

  • In stats, we are also interested in two-tail “intervals”.
    • \(x<15 ~~~\mathtt{OR}~~~ x>35\)

Bounded intervals have a middle (center) and radius

  • In Statistics, we care about distance from center (like in archery, accuracy, error).
  • \(3.5\le x ~~~\mathtt{AND}~~~ x\le 8.5\)

  • Let’s say the boundaries are \(b_1\) and \(b_2\), where \(b_2>b_1\). \[m = \frac{b_1+b_2}{2}\] \[r=\frac{b_2-b_1}{2}\]
  • So, in this example… \[m = \frac{3.5+8.5}{2} = \frac{12}{2} = 6\] \[r = \frac{8.5-3.5}{2} = \frac{5}{2} = 2.5\]

It is helpful to define intervals using middle and radius.

  • \(|x-6|\le 2.5\)

  • \(|x-6|\ge 2.5\)

If you know the middle and radius, you can calculate the bounds

  • Doubly bounded interval: \[|x-m|<r\]
  • Two-tailed interval: \[|x-m|>r\]
  • Absolute difference is 1-dimensional distance. \[|x-m|=\text{distance between }x\text{ and }m \]
  • If you know the middle and radius, you can determine the boundaries. \[b_{1} = m-r \] \[b_{2} = m+r \]
